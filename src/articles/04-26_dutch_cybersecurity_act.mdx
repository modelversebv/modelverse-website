A new legislation - affecting thousands of companies - is soon coming into force in the Netherlands. In the second quarter of 2026, the Dutch Cybersecurity Act (Cyberbeveiligingswet) will be enacted. This is the national implementation of the NIS2 directive, a legal framework requiring member states of the European Union to uphold cybersecurity within crucial sectors. Given the fast-approaching changes, this post discusses the most important elements of the legislation.

## Who does the new Dutch Cybersecurity Act affect?

Unfortunately, there is no quick answer to this question, with the explanation being nuanced and multi-layered. Given the legislation’s complex nature, the government created an [evaluation tool](https://regelhulpenvoorbedrijven.nl/NIS-2-NL/) that companies can use to see whether the new legislation impacts them.

However, fundamentally, the ‘Cyberbeveiligingswet’ affects: entities that are part of specific (sub)sectors (see [attachments 1 and 2](https://www.tweedekamer.nl/kamerstukken/wetsvoorstellen/detail?cfg=wetsvoorsteldetails&qry=wetsvoorstel%3A36764#wetgevingsproces) of the legislation for the detailed lists); essential and important companies; entities who are situated in the Netherlands; and entities providing services or performing activities in the Netherlands or another EU Member State. The difference between an essential and important company depends on the sector in which they operate, their size, and the services they provide. It should be noted that knowing the distinction between these entities is particularly relevant, considering that it impacts how supervision is conducted. Specifically, while supervision of essential companies will be proactive - with the government constantly monitoring them for compliance - the supervision of important companies will mostly be reactive, taking place after incidents or indications of non-compliance with the Act.

## Important things to consider in the ‘Cyberbeveiligingswet’

Firstly, the legislation introduces a duty to report (meldplicht). In its essence, it requires a company to report any incidents that either cause a serious operational disruption or financial loss to the company, or (potentially) affect other companies through causing significant damage. An incident can be reported in 2 ways: via logging into [‘Nationaal Cyber Security Centrum’ (NCSC)](https://mijn.ncsc.nl/), or via a web form. Be aware that the latter option requires more time due to identification details not being pre-filled.

Secondly, the entities will also be required to register themself in a national portal, in which they must provide information such as IP ranges and contact information. While organizations are only required to do this once the Cybersecurity Act comes into force, it is [already possible to do this now](https://mijn.ncsc.nl/) - on a voluntary basis - in order to make the transition easier. However, entities operating in different EU countries should be careful that they register with the appropriate member state(s); this is their responsibility, and failing to do so could lead to unpleasant consequences.

Thirdly, the Cybersecurity Act discusses the duty of care (zorgplicht), with [10 measures](https://www.ncsc.nl/cyberbeveiligingswet-nis2/zorgplicht) being defined for protecting network and information systems, as well as the physical environment of those systems. Some of these measures include supply chain security, business continuity, as well as policies and procedures to assess the effectiveness of cybersecurity risk management measures. It is important to recognize that organizations themselves are responsible for determining which of the measures are appropriate, with regulatory authorities assessing - per entity - if risk management was done correctly and if the implemented measures are sufficient.

Lastly, an important point is made with regards to governance: every member of the company’s board is required to undergo a training and receive a certificate of participation within 2 years of the legislation’s entry into force. The training must cover subjects to prove that the board members have certain knowledge and skills about risks of network and information systems, as well as risk management measures. And yes, even if a board member thinks that they have enough knowledge, they must still do the training. However, the government does not provide a list of approved training providers, with individuals being free to choose where they take the training.

While this concise overview is not exhaustive, it gives some insight into the changes taking place and steps that entities will have to take in order to be compliant.

## To finish off: what can companies do right now?

The government recommends 4 steps that companies can already take to prepare themselves for the changes.

1. Identify whether your company falls under the new ‘Cyberbeveiligingswet’. For that you can use the Dutch [NIS2 self-evaluation tool](https://regelhulpenvoorbedrijven.nl/NIS-2-NL/).
2. Rate your cyber security controls and identify your improvement points. The ‘Cyberbeveiligingswet’ [Control Framework](https://www.auditdienstrijk.nl/documenten/2025/september/30/cbw-nis2-control-framework) and the [NIS2 Quickscan](https://regelhulpenvoorbedrijven.nl/NIS2-Quickscan/) can be used for this.
3. Register your company with the [NCSC](https://mijn.ncsc.nl/).
4. Begin with incorporating the 10 measures of care. The NCSC gives an [overview of these measures](https://www.ncsc.nl/cyberbeveiligingswet-nis2/bereid-je-voor/zorgplicht).
